Method.

To review, we apply the following method to study 2D nonlinear dynamical systems.
  1. Write a model.
  2. Read and interpret the model (dimensional analysis of parameters.
  3. Compute nullclines and equilibria.
  4. Plot phase portraits with direction arrows and trajectories.
  5. Refine knowledge of stability with linearization via Jacobian.
Consider two species \(A(t)\) and \(B(t)\) for ants and beetles, respectively. Assume that each grows (in the absence of the other) according to logistic growth,
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \begin{cases} \displaystyle\frac{d^{} {A}}{d {t}^{}}&=a A\Big(1-\frac{A}{K_A}\Big)\\[10pt] \displaystyle\frac{d^{} {B}}{d {t}^{}}&=b B\Big(1-\frac{B}{K_B}\Big). \end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Here, each species grows independently of the other. Beetles have no effect on ants; ants have no effect on beetles. Our mechanism for interaction will be interference competition, which leads in increased mortality.
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \begin{cases} \displaystyle\frac{d^{} {A}}{d {t}^{}}&=a A\Big(1-\frac{A}{K_A}-\frac{\mu B}{K_A}\Big)\\[10pt] \displaystyle\frac{d^{} {B}}{d {t}^{}}&=b B\Big(1-\frac{B}{K_B}-\frac{\delta A}{K_B}\Big). \end{cases}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}
To understand interspecific competition, we need to understand the units and magnitude of \(\mu\) and \(\delta\text{.}\) Roughly, \(\mu\) translates from units of beetle density to an “effective ant density.” According to the ratio \(\frac{B}{K_A}\text{,}\) beetles ‘fill’ some of the carrying capacity allotted to ants. Just how much of the capacity that they fill is given by the parameter \(\mu\text{,}\) which when multiplied by \(B\) gives the ‘ant equivalency’ of \(B\) beetles. We will look more closely at the equation for \(B\text{,}\) but note that we could make similar observations about the equation for \(A\text{.}\)
The dynamics of \(B\) are given by,
\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {B}}{d {t}^{}}=b B\Big(1-\frac{B+\delta A}{K_B}\Big), \end{equation*}
which slightly more directly illustrates that both \(A\) and \(B\) fill the carrying capacity allotted to \(B\text{.}\) In one dimension, equilibria depend on the zeros of the right hand side of an equation like this. In two dimensions, equilibria depend on the values of both quantities \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) The first step, as before, is to compute the nullclines for \(B\text{.}\) Setting \(\frac{d^{} {B}}{d {t}^{}}=0\) implies that either \(B=0\) or
\begin{equation*} 1-\frac{B+\delta A}{K_B}=0. \end{equation*}
With a single species, this equation looks like
\begin{equation*} 1-\frac{\text{animals}}{K_B}=0. \end{equation*}
We can solve this equation for the density of animals at equilibrium to find,
\begin{equation*} \text{animals}=K_B. \end{equation*}
But we know that we have a particular composition of animals, such that
\begin{equation*} \text{animals}=B+\delta A. \end{equation*}
Combining these equations,
\begin{equation*} K_B=B+\delta A, \end{equation*}
gives \(B\) as a function of \(A\text{,}\) the abundance of \(A\) and \(B\) that guarantee that \(B\) is unchanging,
\begin{equation*} B=K_B -\delta A. \end{equation*}
Finally, we have that \(\dfrac{dB}{dt}=0\text{,}\) if either \(B=0\) or \(B = K_B - \delta A\text{.}\) By their definition, on \(B\)-nullclines, the motion in the \(B\) direction is zero, and dynamics are strictly increasing (or decreasing) in \(A\text{.}\)
Similarly, we can compute that \(\dfrac{dA}{dt}=0\text{,}\) if either \(A=0\) or \(B = \dfrac{K_A - A}{\mu}\text{.}\) By their definition, on \(A\)-nullclines, the motion in the \(A\) direction is zero, and dynamics are strictly increasing (or decreasing) in \(B\text{.}\) We have to consider a variety of parameter combinations and relative magnitudes.
