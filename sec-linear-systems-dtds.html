    Section 3.2 Linear systems of difference equations

    Linear systems of difference equations are quite applicable, and moreover are very interesting examples of some key principles of linear algebra. For a very brief review of linear algebra, check out Section A.1.

    Example 3.2.1.

    Consider the following model of seedling/adult (i.e., vegetative and reproductive) plant dynamics of the form \(P_{t+1} = MP_t\text{.}\) Entries of the matrix will be identified below, but to start assume the population starts with an initial distribution of \(P_0= \begin{pmatrix} V_0\\R_0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 100\\0 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\)
    Take a population with
    \begin{equation*} M=M_w = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \amp s_w\\\sigma_w \amp a_w\end{pmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where the subscript \(w\) denotes a population that experiences `wet’ weather. Let \(s_w=1.50\text{,}\) \(\sigma_w = 0.55\text{,}\) and \(a_w=0.1\text{.}\) On average, adults (reproductive) one year give rise to one and a half new seedlings (vegetative) the next, just over half of the seedlings in one year survive to adulthood the next year, but only one in ten adults survives year to year.
    • Compute a few values of the solution from initial conditions \(V_0 = 100\) and \(R_0 = 0\text{.}\)
    • Compute eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the matrix \(M_w\text{.}\)
    • Express the solution for these initial conditions in terms of eigenvectors and eigenvalues.

    Example 3.2.2.

    Consider the following model of seedling/adult (i.e., vegetative and reproductive) plant dynamics of the form \(P_{t+1} = MP_t\text{.}\) Entries of the matrix will be identified below, but to start assume the population starts with an initial distribution of \(P_0=\begin{pmatrix} V_0\\R_0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 100\\0 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) Take a population with
    \begin{equation*} M=M_d = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \amp s_w\\\sigma_w \amp a_w\end{pmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where the subscript \(d\) denotes a population that experiences `wet’ weather. Let \(s_d=2.50\text{,}\) \(\sigma_d = 0.05\text{,}\) and \(a_d=0.8\text{.}\) On average, adults (reproductive) one year give rise to one and a half new seedlings (vegetative) the next, just over half of the seedlings in one year survive to adulthood the next year, but only one in ten adults survives year to year.
    • Compute a few values of the solution from initial conditions \(V_0 = 100\) and \(R_0 = 0\text{.}\)
    • Compute eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the matrix \(M_d\text{.}\)
    • Express the solution for these initial conditions in terms of eigenvectors and eigenvalues.

    Example 3.2.3.

    Now for a twist. Assume that plants experience variable weather, specifically alternating wet and dry years. For example, \(P_1 = M_wP_0\text{,}\) followed by \(P_2 = M_dP_1\text{.}\) We might use the 2-year updating matrix \(M_{dw} = M_d M_w\) and measure our plants every two years. Let \(Z_t\) denote the \(t\)-th measurement (i.e., made so that \(Z_0 = P_0\text{,}\) \(Z_1 = P_2\text{,}\) \(Z_2 = P_4\text{,}\) and so on), described by
    \begin{equation*} Z_{t+1} = M_{dw} Z_t\text{.} \end{equation*}
    • Compute a few values of the solution from initial conditions \(V_0 = 100\) and \(R_0 = 0\text{.}\)
    • Compute eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the matrix \(M_{dw}\text{.}\)
    • Express the solution for these initial conditions in terms of eigenvectors and eigenvalues.
    • What do you notice about high powers of each eigenvalue?
