Back to our example
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{pmatrix} 5\amp3\\2\amp6\end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
so first \(\operatorname{det}(A) = (5)(6) - (3)(2) = 24\text{.}\) Now,
\begin{equation*} A^{-1} = \nicefrac{1}{24}\begin{pmatrix} 6\amp-3\\-2\amp5\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \nicefrac{6}{24}\amp\nicefrac{-3}{24}\\\nicefrac{-2}{24}\ amp\nicefrac{5}{24}\end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
The coefficients could be simplified, but, why bother? To solve our original problem, we multiply both sides by the inverse of \(A\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} \nicefrac{6}{24}\amp\nicefrac{-3}{24}\\\nicefrac{-2}{ 24}\amp\nicefrac{5}{24}\end{pmatrix}\cdot \begin{pmatrix} 5\amp3\\2\amp6\end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} x\\y\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \nicefrac{6}{24}\amp\nicefrac{-3}{24}\\\nicefrac{-2}{24}\ amp\nicefrac{5}{24}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 26\\20\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
The left, for reasons we can confirm later, is simply \(\begin{pmatrix} x\\y\end{pmatrix}\text{,}\) while on the right we have to calculate the result of matrix-vector multiplication,
\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} \nicefrac{6}{24}\amp\nicefrac{-3}{24}\\ \nicefrac{-2}{24} \amp \nicefrac{5}{24} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 26\\20 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} (\nicefrac{6}{24})(26) + (\nicefrac{-3}{24})(20)\\ (\nicefrac{-2}{24})(26) + (\nicefrac{5}{24})(20) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4\\2 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
To confirm what happens on the left,
\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} \nicefrac{6}{24}\amp\nicefrac{-3}{24}\\ \nicefrac{-2}{24}\amp\nicefrac{5}{24} \end{pmatrix}\cdot \begin{pmatrix} 5\amp3\\ 2\amp6 \end{pmatrix} \amp = \begin{pmatrix} (\nicefrac{6}{24})(5) + (\nicefrac{-3}{24})(2) \amp (\nicefrac{6}{24})(3) + (\nicefrac{-3}{24})(6)\\ \nicefrac{-2}{24})(5) + (\nicefrac{5}{24})(2) \amp (\nicefrac{-2}{24})(3) + (\nicefrac{5}{24})(6) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
