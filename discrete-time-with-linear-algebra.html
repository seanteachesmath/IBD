    Section 7.1 Discrete-time dynamical systems with linear algebra

    We spent some time on the Nicholson-Bailey model for a few important reasons: largely because it is a great example of the modeling process. The results it generates may be bothersome. But the idea that we told a rough story, then iteratively refined it is the art of mathematical modeling.
    Recall our example concerning weekly dandelion dynamics. Previously, we counted only the number of flowering plants present in our ‚Äòyard‚Äô each week. We can count a bit more carefully, and take advantage of more biological information if we think about the ‚Äôstructure‚Äô of the population. Ignoring seeds, in reality we have at least a few ways to break down the plant community:
    • juveniles vs. adults
    • seedlings vs. vegetative vs. flowering vs. seed-setting
    • non-reproductive vs. reproductive
    We will lump together the seedlings and vegetative plans as ‚Äònon-reproductive‚Äô and the flowering and seed-setting plants as ‚Äòreproductive‚Äô. Let \(V_t\) be the number of non-reproductive (vegetative) plants in the yard and \(R_t\) be the number of reproductive plants in the yard, both at week \(t\text{.}\)
    We will use familiar notation for parameters. Since it is challenging to count seeds after they have left the plant, and nobody vacuums seeds from the yard, we‚Äôll focus on the life stages above.
    This means we can write
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} V_{t+1}&=0\cdot V_t+s\cdot R_t\\ R_{t+1}&=\sigma \cdot V_t+ a\cdot R_t. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    This looks like a matrix equation,
    \begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{c} V_{t+1}\\ R_{t+1}\end{array} \right) = \left( \begin{array}{cc} 0 & s\\ \sigma &a \end{array} \right)\left( \begin{array}{c}V_{t}\\ R_{t} \end{array} \right). \end{equation*}
    With a single equation, it was easy enough to determine growth. This is less obvious with a higher dimensional model. Additionally, we might be interested in the following questions:
    • Will the population grow?
    • If it grows, how fast is it going to grow?
    • What is the balance between reproductive and non-reproductive dandelions in the population?
    For a plant starting in the non-reproductive state (a one week old plant), we track its lifetime reproduction in the following table:
    Next we append a column for the reproductive value of each age (remembering that our model only tracks two age classes, but once plants flower they could survive indefinitely):
    Starting from seedling, the expected lifetime reproduction of a plant is
    \begin{equation*} ELR=s\sigma+s\sigma a + s\sigma a^2 + \dots . \end{equation*}
    By definition \(a\) is a weekly survival probability for adult plants, so \(a\leq1\text{.}\) Keep this in mind as we press on:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ELR & = s\sigma+s\sigma a + s\sigma a^2 + \dots \\ & = s\sigma\left(1+a+a^2+\dots\right)\\ & = s\sigma \sum_{k=0}^\infty a^k \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Knowing what we do about geometric series,
    \begin{equation*} ELR = s\sigma \sum_{k=0}^\infty a^k = s\sigma\left(\frac{1}{1-a}\right). \end{equation*}
    So, \(ELR >1\) when \(s\sigma>1-a\text{.}\) This is identical to our ‚Äòadult plant‚Äô-only model result,
    \begin{equation*} s\sigma+a>1! \end{equation*}

    Questions.

    • What‚Äôs so special about ELR as a measure of growth?
    • How does ELR relate to eigenvalues?

    Answers.

    For a matrix \(A\text{,}\) like our transition matrix above, a number \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue with eigenvector \(x\) if
    \begin{equation*} Ax=\lambda x. \end{equation*}
    The eigenvalue reduces the dimension in multidimensional problems. It turns matrix multiplication into scalar multiplication. How do we find eigenvalues?

    Linear algebra review.

    We first want to write our eigenvalue problem as:
    \begin{equation*} Ax-\lambda x=0. \end{equation*}
    Then, since \(\lambda\) is a number,
    \begin{equation*} (A-\lambda I)x=0, \end{equation*}
    where \(I\) is the appropriate ‚Äòidentity matrix‚Äô.
    Let‚Äôs take
    \begin{equation*} A= \left( \begin{array}{cc} a & b\\ c & d \end{array} \right), \end{equation*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} A-\lambda I= \left( \begin{array}{cc} a-\lambda & b\\ c & d-\lambda \end{array} \right). \end{equation*}
    Eigenvalues solve
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{det}(A-\lambda I)=0. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 0&=\operatorname{det}(A-\lambda I)\\ &=(a-\lambda)(d-\lambda)-bc\\ 0&=\lambda^2-(a+d)\lambda+ad-bc \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Solutions are
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_{\pm}=\frac{(a+d)\pm\sqrt{(a+ d)^2-4(ad-bc)}}{2}. \end{equation*}
    From here it helps to consider specific examples so we do not have to solve equations containing obtuse algebraic expressions.

    Subsection 7.1.1 Linear discrete-time models (2D)

    Question.

    How do structured and unstructured populations differ? From a one dimensional model ignoring plant developmental stage,
    \begin{equation*} N_t=r N_t, \end{equation*}
    where \(r=s\sigma+a\text{,}\) we wrote a two-dimensional model,
    \begin{equation*} P_{t+1}=MP_t, \end{equation*}
    where \(P_0=(V_0, R_0)^T\) and
    \begin{equation*} M=\left( \begin{array}{cc}0 & s\\\sigma & a\end{array}\right). \end{equation*}
    Keeping with our example, eigenvalues of \(M\) are found using
    \begin{equation*} \det\left( \begin{array}{cc} -\lambda & s\\ \sigma & a-\lambda \end{array} \right)=0. \end{equation*}
    This gives
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 0&=-\lambda(a-\lambda)-s\sigma\\ &=\lambda^2-a\lambda-s\sigma \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Using the quadratic formula,
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_\pm = \frac{a\pm\sqrt{a^2+4s\sigma}}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Suppose we know the initial plant distribution,
    \begin{equation*} P_0=\left( \begin{array}{c} V_0\\ R_0 \end{array} \right), \end{equation*}
    the solution to our model is
    \begin{equation*} P_t=M^tP_0. \end{equation*}
    First write, \(P_0=c_1 v_1+c_2 v_2\text{,}\) where \(c_1, c_2\) are constants and \(v_1, v_2\) are eigenvectors. We are assuming that \(v_1, v_2\) are linearly independent, thus they form a basis in two-dimensions, such that any other vector can be written as a linear combination.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_1&=A P_0 & \quad\text{(start)}\\ & = A(c_1 v_1+c_2 v_2)& \quad\text{(basis vectors)}\\ & = c_1 Av_1+c_2 Av_2& \quad\text{(distributive property)}\\ P_1& = c_1 \lambda_1 v_1+c_2 \lambda_2v_2& \quad\text{(eigenvalues!)} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    We can compute \(P_2\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_2&=A P_1\\ & = A(c_1 \lambda_1 v_1+c_2 \lambda_2 v_2)\\ & = (c_1 \lambda_1) Av_1+(c_2\lambda_2) Av_2\\ & = c_1 \lambda_1^2 v_1+c_2 \lambda_2^2v_2 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    And similarly,
    \begin{equation*} P_t=c_1\lambda_1^tv_1+c_2\lambda_2^tv_2. \end{equation*}
    If we assume that \(|\lambda_1|>|\lambda_2|\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} P_t\approx c_1 \lambda_1^t v_1. \end{equation*}
    We will see how this approximation works at the end of the following example. But, perhaps motivated by this approximation, we see that the magnitude of \(\lambda_1\) is important to the growth of the population, both \(V_t\) and \(R_t\text{.}\) The population changes by a factor \(\lambda_1\) each generation. It grows if \(\lambda_1>1\) and shrinks if \(\lambda_1<1\text{.}\)
    Let \(\lambda_1\) be the positive eigenvalue, \(\lambda_1>1\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_1 =\frac{a+\sqrt{a^2+4s\sigma}}{2}&>1\\ a+\sqrt{a^2+4s\sigma}&>2\\ \sqrt{a^2+4s\sigma}&>2-a\\ a^2+4s\sigma&>(2-a)^2\\ a^2+4s\sigma&>4-4a+a^2\\ 4s\sigma&>4-4a\\ s\sigma&>1-a\\ s\sigma+a&>1 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    The positive eigenvalue \(\lambda_1>1\) if \(r=s\sigma+a>1\text{,}\) but in general, \(ELR\neq\lambda_1\text{!}\) But, if \(\lambda_1>1\) so is \(ELR\text{,}\) and vice versa. Again, our characteristic polynomial, that governs the eigenvalues is
    \begin{equation*} C(\lambda)=\lambda^2-a\lambda-s\sigma. \end{equation*}
    Since \(s, \sigma>0\text{,}\) we have \(C(0)<0\) and we know that \(C(\lambda)\) is concave up. If \(C(1)<0\text{,}\) we know that the leading eigenvalue \(\lambda>1\text{.}\) Lastly, let‚Äôs study the relationships between \(1, \lambda_+\text{,}\) and \(r\text{.}\) We‚Äôve already looked at some of the relationships involving \(1\text{.}\) Now, \(\lambda<r=s\sigma+a\) when
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 0&<C(r)\\ 0&<r^2-ar-s\sigma\\ s\sigma&<r(r-a)\\ s\sigma&<(s\sigma+a)(s\sigma+a-a)\\ s\sigma&<s\sigma(s\sigma+a)\\ 0&<s\sigma(s\sigma+a)-s\sigma\\ 0&<s\sigma(s\sigma+a-1) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    This is satisfied when \(r=s\sigma+a>1\text{,}\) thus \(1<\lambda<ELR\text{.}\) For the reverse, \(ELR<\lambda<1\) when \(r=s\sigma+a<1\text{.}\) We will also look at the relationships between \(r\text{,}\) \(ELR\text{,}\) and \(\lambda\) in the example, so that we can actually compute eigenpair.

    Example 7.1.4. A matrix model.

    Recall \(r=s\sigma+a\) was the per generation growth rate from the one-equation model. In that model, we made no distinction between plant growth stages, an assumed that all plants were capable of reproducing. When we considered the reproductive schedule of our plants, we derived \(ELR\) as a measure of the replacement rate of each individual plant. Let \(a=\frac{2}{3}\text{,}\) \(s=\frac{16}{9}\text{,}\) \(\sigma=\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) so that, \(r=\left(\frac{16}{9}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)+ \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)= \left(\frac{14}{9}\right)\approx1.\overline{55}\text{.}\) Also, \(ELR=\frac{\left(\frac{16}{9}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)}{\frac{1}{3}}=\frac{8}{3}\approx2.\overline{66}\text{.}\) When vegetative plants are less likely to mature (small \(\sigma\text{,}\)¬†Figure¬†7.1.5) weekly adult survivorship, \(a\text{,}\) must increase in order for population growth to occur (\(r, ELR, \lambda>1\)).

    Question.

    What is the effect of population structure (tracking flower types), in growing and shrinking flower populations (see¬†Figure¬†7.1.5)?

    Answer.

    Imposing structure reduces the growth rate (relative to \(r\)) in growing populations. Imposing structure slows the decay rate (relative to \(r\)) in shrinking populations. In this parameterization,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_\pm &=\frac{\frac{2}{3}\pm\sqrt{\frac{4}{9}+4\left(\frac{16}{9}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) }}{2}\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\pm\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{4 }{9}+\frac{32}{9}}\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\pm 1\\ \lambda_\pm&=\frac{4}{3}, -\frac{2}{3}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{cc} 0 &\frac{16}{9}\\[2pt] \frac{1}{2} &\frac{2}{3} \end{array}\right) \left( \begin{array}{c} x_1\\ x_2 \end{array} \right)= \frac{4}{3}\left( \begin{array}{c} x_1\\ x_2 \end{array} \right). \end{equation*}
    Notice that \(ELR>\lambda_+>1\text{,}\) so that the population grows.
    The eigenvector associated with \(\lambda=\frac{4}{3}\) is of the form
    \begin{equation*} v_1=\left( \begin{array}{c}x_1\\ \frac{3}{4}x_1\end{array}\right). \end{equation*}
    The eigenvector associated with \(\lambda=-\frac{2}{3}\) is of the form
    \begin{equation*} v_1=\left( \begin{array}{c}x_1\\ -\frac{3}{8}x_1\end{array}\right). \end{equation*}

    Question.

    How do the eigenvectors help explain long term population growth (see¬†Figure¬†7.1.7)?

    Answer.

    Long-term proportions approach the distribution described by the leading eigenvector. Numbers increase geometrically. Start with a typical initial condition \(P_0=(V_0, R_0)=(0,1)\text{.}\) We first want to express \(P_0\) as a linear combination of eigenvectors.
    \begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \end{array} \right) = c_1\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ \frac{3}{4} \end{array} \right)+c_2\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ -\frac{3}{8} \end{array} \right). \end{equation*}
    Skipping a few calculations, \(c_1=-c_2\) and \(c_1=\frac{8}{9}\text{.}\) Finally we can write,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_1&=A P_0\\ & = A\left(c_1\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ \frac{3}{4} \end{array} \right)+c_2 \left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ -\frac{3}{8} \end{array} \right)\right)\\ & = c_1 A\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ \frac{3}{4} \end{array}\right)+c_2 A\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ -\frac{3}{8} \end{array} \right)\\ P_1& = c_1 \lambda_1^2 \left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ \frac{3}{4} \end{array}\right)+c_2 \lambda_2^2\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ -\frac{3}{8} \end{array}\right)\\ P_1& = \left( \frac{8}{9}\right) \left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^2 \left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ \frac{3}{4} \end{array}\right)- \left(\frac{8}{9}\right) \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^2\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ -\frac{3}{8} \end{array}\right), \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    as before. Iterating this solution we wind up with,
    \begin{equation*} P_t=\frac{8}{9}\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^t\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ \frac{3}{4} \end{array} \right)- \frac{8}{9}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^t\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ -\frac{3}{8} \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}
    Since the second term contains a value \(\lambda_2<1\) which is raised to a larger and larger power, its contribution decreases. If we ignore it (at large \(t\)),
    \begin{equation*} P_t\approx\frac{8}{9}\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^t\left( \begin{array}{c} 1\\ \frac{3}{4} \end{array} \right). \end{equation*}
    The population will grow 33% larger each generation, but the proportion of young (non-reproductive) and old (reproductive) plants will remain fixed. This suggests that juvenile (tasty young plants) will outnumber the older flowering plants, specifically, the flowering plants will be 75% the abundance of young plants. Alternatively, young plants will be 33% more abundant than old plants. It might also be useful to use our leading eigenvector approximation, rather than our exact matrix solution.
