    Section 5.2 Introduction to stochastic models

    Motivation.

    So far we have worked with deterministic models, most recently in continuous time. An example of a deterministic model is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}=rN, \end{equation*}
    where \(r\) is the growth rate of species \(N\text{.}\) The solution to such a model is
    \begin{equation*} N(t)=N_0e^{rt}, \end{equation*}
    where \(N_0\) is the initial population size. As \(t\rightarrow\infty\text{,}\) we have either \(N\rightarrow 0\) if \(b<m\) (\(r<0\)), or \(N\rightarrow \infty\) if \(b>m\) (\(r>0\)). In decaying populations \(r<0\)), solutions decay exponentially towards zero, rather than actually reaching zero. Thus, we reach extinction asymptotically, rather than in finite time.
    But, at low numbers chance events become important. A single cell could divide or die. If it dies, we have extinction. If \(r<0\) we expect extinction, but what about when \(r>0\text{?}\) If \(r>0\text{,}\) a single cell could die rather than divide, by chance, even if it should divide and grow in number.
    Since we are focusing on the fates of individual cells, it is perhaps important to think in terms of integer-valued states. Let \(N_0=n\) be the initial number of cells present, perhaps in come cell culture of a fascinating biological experiment. We should note that, while the process is discrete (in the number of cells), time is continuous. The number of animals changes by a single individual through births (to the right) and deaths (to the left) as illustrated in¬†Figure¬†5.2.1.
    To make use of the diagram in Figure¬†5.2.1 we should let
    \begin{equation*} P_n(t)=\text{Prob}(\text{counting \(n\) animals at time \(t\)}), \end{equation*}
    and consider how these probabilities evolve in time for all \(n\text{.}\) One important fact is that
    \begin{equation*} P_i(0)=\begin{cases}1, \,\text{if \(i=n\)}\\0, \,\text{otherwise}\end{cases}, \end{equation*}
    since we know that we start from \(N_0=n\) animals with absolute certainty. We can derive the differential equation by conservation,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_n(t+\Delta t) & =\text{Prob(a birth)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n-1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\\ & +\text{Prob(a death)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n+1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\\ & +\text{Prob(no change)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n\) animals at time \(t\)}) && \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    First we will apply the definition of \(P_n(t)\) and the others (i.e., \(P_{n-1}(t)\)). Notice that the equation is presented in two columns, on the left is the format ‚Äòin words‚Äô and on the right introduces the corresponding notation,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_n(t+\Delta t) & =\text{Prob(a birth)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n-1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad=\text{Prob(a birth)}\times P_{n-1}(t) \\ & +\text{Prob(no change)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad+\text{Prob(no change)}\times P_{n}(t) & +\text{Prob(a death)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n+1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad+\text{Prob(a death)}\times P_{n+1}(t)\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Below we could also consider \(\text{Prob(no change)}\) as \((1-\text{Prob(change)})\text{,}\) where \(\text{Prob(change)}\) is given by the probability of events resulting in exit from size \(n\text{.}\) The event probabilities are,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_n(t+\Delta t) & =\text{Prob(a birth)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n-1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad=(n-1)b\Delta t P_{n-1}(t) \\ & +\text{Prob(no change)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad+(1-nb\Delta t-n\mu\Delta t) P_{n}(t) & +\text{Prob(a death)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n+1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad+(n+1)\mu\Delta t P_{n+1}(t)\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Finally, we can begin rearranging to derive the differential equation for \(P_n(t)\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_n(t+\Delta t) &=(n-1)b\Delta t P_{n-1}(t) +(n+1)\mu\Delta t P_{n+1}(t)+(1-nb\Delta t-n\mu\Delta t) P_{n}(t) \\ P_n(t+\Delta t) -P_n(t)&=(n-1)b\Delta t P_{n-1}(t) +(n+1)\mu\Delta t P_{n+1}(t)-(nb\Delta t+n\mu\Delta t) P_{n}(t) \\ \frac{P_n(t+\Delta t) -P_n(t)}{\Delta t}&=(n-1)b P_{n-1}(t) +(n+1)\mu P_{n+1}(t)-n(b + \mu) P_{n}(t) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Now, taking the limit \(\Delta t\rightarrow0\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {P_n(t)}}{d {t}^{}}=(n-1)b P_{n-1}(t) +(n+1)\mu P_{n+1}(t)-(nb-n\mu) P_{n}(t) . \end{equation*}
    We have to assume that \(P_i(t)=0\) for any \(i<0\text{,}\) and accept a system of coupled equations for \(n=0, 1, 2, \dots\text{.}\) We will study this particular model with a suite of techniques, including naive calculation, inspection, and somewhat elegant probability theory.

    Naive calculation.

    Looking at the first equation for \(n=0\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {P_0(t)}}{d {t}^{}}=(-1)b P_{-1}(t) +(1)\mu P_{1}(t)-0\cdot (b -\mu) P_{0}(t), \end{equation*}
    we simplify to
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {P_0(t)}}{d {t}^{}}= \mu P_{1}(t). \end{equation*}
    If we were to look at the equilibrium of \(P_0\text{,}\) assuming \(\mu\neq0\text{,}\) we would need \(P_1(t)\rightarrow0\text{.}\) Since \(P_1\) appears in the next equation, we require all \(P_i(t)\rightarrow0\) for \(i>0\text{.}\) Since \(P_i(t)\) are probabilities, we require \(\displaystyle\sum_0^\infty P_i(t)=1\text{,}\) which means that \(P_0(t)\rightarrow1\text{.}\) This suggests that extinction is unavoidable, but a bit more work provides additional insight. Specifically populations with \(b>m\) can escape extinction, with the probability of extinction of
    \begin{equation*} \left(\dfrac{m}{b}\right)^{N_0} \end{equation*}
    which for \(b>m\) decays geometrically as a function of the initial population size. With quite a bit of effort, this result is very nicely illustrated in classic texts.
