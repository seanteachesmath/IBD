We can introduce a matrix-vector equation
\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 5\amp 3\\ 2\amp 6 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} x\\y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 26\\20 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
We take the numbers in the first row, rotate 90 degrees, align with the \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) multiply and add, in order to get back the entry of the first row on the right-hand side, here \(26\text{.}\) Above when dealing with \emph{scalar} variables like \(x\) and \(y\) whose coefficients were scalars, we could eliminate multiplication with division.
