Review: p.c.p. vs. the updating function.

Multiplying \((p.c.p.)\text{,}\) which is a function of \(N_t\text{,}\) by population size \(N_t\) gives our updating function, \(f(N_t)=(p.c.p.)\times N_t\text{.}\) Then, our model is
\begin{equation*} N_{t+1}=f(N_t)=(p.c.p.)\times N_t. \end{equation*}
Some sample per capita production curves and the corresponding updating functions are plotted in Figure 6.3.1. Notice that we explicitly ignore immigration here.
Notice that there are some major qualitative changes when we compare the lines in the left panel to any of the curves in the right panel. Most importantly, the nonlinear updating function intersects the resetting line more than once. Again, at these intersections
\begin{equation*} N_{t+1}=N_t=N^*. \end{equation*}
This suggests an alternative method to identify equilibria, which might sometimes be pictured more simply:
Equilibria satisfy \(N_{t+1}=N_t,\) and we denote them by \(N^*=N_{t+1}=N_t\text{.}\) Given a model \(N_{t+1}=f(N_t)N_t\text{,}\) we can see that equilibria occur at points where \(f(N_t)=1\text{.}\) At values of \(N_t\) where our per capita production takes a value of one, we have equilibria.
  • \(a\) is ‘too steep’ at \(G^*\) perturbations near \(G^*\) grow, so solutions diverge from \(G^*\text{.}\)
  • \(a\) is ‘shallow enough’ at \(G^*\) perturbations near \(G^*\) decay, so solutions converge to \(G^*\text{.}\)
From our linear theory, we know that \(x_t\rightarrow0\) when \(|a|<1\text{.}\)
