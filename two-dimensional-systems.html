    As we transition towards new topics, it is worth mentioning our goals.
    • Read and interpret models A term by term understanding of the assumptions of a model.
    • Analyze, ‚Äòsolve,‚Äô or simulate (compute) solutions. Equilibria and stability, or transient dynamics.
    • Write models.

    A disease model.

    Consider a mild infection circulating among members of a population, where the ‚Äòhealthy‚Äô individuals are susceptible to the infection (\(S\)) and the ‚Äòinfectious‚Äô individuals are capable of transmitting the infection to others (\(I\)).
    Much like in class we will build a word model as we go.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \text{change in }S & = +\text{(gain from recovery)} - \text{(loss to infection)}\\ \text{change in }I & = +\text{(gain from infection)} - \text{(loss to recovery)}, \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    and replace the terms with symbols. Our ‚Äòfirst‚Äô term is ‚Äòloss to recovery‚Äô in the \(I\) equation. We can think of \(\delta\) as the leaving rate from the \(I\) compartment, much like \(m\) was the ‚Äòleaving rate from the living‚Äô (i.e., dying) compartment of a population model. Everything, in this model, that leaves one compartment enters another (i.e., there are no ‚Äòleaks‚Äô). We can also argue for the infection rate as follows, since infection is only transmitted directly and by infectious hosts, we might expect that the rate that \(S\) individuals gain infection is proportional to the density (individuals per hectare) of infectious individuals, \(I\text{.}\) In fact here, we assume that the infection rate is a linearly increasing function of \(I\text{.}\) There are plenty of other models of transmission, some we will study in the homework, others later in the semester, still more in our projects.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \text{change in }S & = +\text{(gain from recovery)} &&-\text{(loss to infection)}\\ \frac{dS}{dt}&= + \delta I && - {\beta I} S\\ \text{change in }I & = + \text{(gain from infection)}&& -\text{(loss to recovery)}\\ \frac{dI}{dt}&= + \beta IS && -{\delta} I \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Since the total number* of individuals \(N\) is preserved, we can replace the equation for \(S\) by an algebraic constraint \(S=N-I\text{.}\) We can substitute this into the equation for \(I\) and derive a 1-dimensional, nonlinear model,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {I}}{d {t}^{}}=\beta I (N-I)-\delta I. \end{equation*}
    If we define our model \(h(I)=\beta I (N-I)-\delta I,\) we can plot the more interesting version of our phase line as in [cross-reference to target(s) "fig-phase" missing or not unique]. Ignoring graphs for the moment, we can also compute equilibria of continuous time models, defined as solutions to \(h(I^*)=0\text{.}\) This gives \(I^*=0\) and \(I^*=N-\frac{\delta}{\beta}.\) It might prove useful to notice that our nontrivial solution exists when \(\delta<\beta N\) this suggests a limitation on the recovery rate that depends on the contact parameter \(\beta\) and the total population size \(N\text{.}\)
